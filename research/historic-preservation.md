# Historic Preservation Constraints: 19 Linden St

## National Register of Historic Places

- **Listed:** October 17, 1985
- **Reference:** NR #85003284
- **Significance:** Best-preserved survivor of the 1843 "Lindens" subdivision — Brookline's first planned garden suburb, designed by Thomas Aspinwall Davis

### What the National Register Does NOT Do

Per the National Park Service: listing "places no restrictions on what a non-federal owner may do with their property up to and including destruction, unless the property is involved in a project that receives Federal assistance."

**Key point:** The National Register alone does NOT prevent demolition, alteration, or redevelopment of privately owned properties. Only projects involving federal funding or federal permits trigger Section 106 review.

A privately funded residential development with no federal involvement would NOT trigger Section 106.

### What the National Register DOES Do

- Makes the property eligible for **Federal Historic Tax Credits** (20% of qualified rehabilitation costs for income-producing use)
- Makes the property eligible for **Massachusetts Historic Tax Credits**
- Triggers the enhanced definition of "demolition" under Brookline's local by-law (see below)

---

## Local Historic District Status

**19 Linden Street is NOT in a Local Historic District.**

Brookline's nine LHDs: Cottage Farm, Pill Hill, Graffam-McKay, Chestnut Hill North, Harvard Avenue, Lawrence, Wild-Sargent, Crowninshield, Olmsted-Richardson.

**No LHD is currently proposed** for the Linden Street area.

This is significant: if the property were in an LHD, the Preservation Commission could **deny** demolition and require approval of all exterior alterations. Without LHD status, the Commission's authority is limited to the demolition delay.

## Brookline Demolition Delay By-Law (Article 5.3)

This is the **primary local constraint** on development.

### How It Works

1. Owner applies for demolition permit
2. Preservation Commission staff makes initial determination within 10 business days
3. If "significant," full Commission holds a public hearing
4. Commission can impose an **18-month delay** for National/State Register properties (12 months for others)
5. After the delay expires, the demolition permit **must be issued**

### Definition of "Demolition" (Enhanced for NR Properties)

For National Register properties, "demolition" includes:
- Removing or covering 25%+ of the exterior sides and roof
- Removing or covering one entire side
- Removing the roof or significantly altering the roof shape
- **Systematic removal, effacement, or destruction of exterior architectural elements** which define or contribute to the historic character — including windows, siding, chimneys, and decorative elements

This broad definition means that even substantial renovation (not just teardown) could trigger the delay.

### Practical Implications

| Action | Triggers Demo Delay? |
|---|---|
| Full demolition | Yes — 18 months |
| Remove 25%+ of exterior | Yes — 18 months |
| Replace windows, siding, chimneys | Possibly — if deemed "systematic removal of character-defining elements" |
| Build new construction behind existing house (adaptive reuse) | Likely no — if existing house is preserved |
| Interior renovation only | No |

## Strategic Implications

### Adaptive Reuse Advantages
1. **Avoids or shortens demolition delay** — preserving the structure means no "demolition"
2. **Qualifies for Historic Tax Credits** — 20% federal + state credits on rehabilitation costs
3. **Reduces community opposition** — Brookline residents care deeply about this building
4. **Creates marketing differentiation** — "live in a landmark" is a powerful selling point
5. **Politically easier** with Planning and Preservation Commission

### Full Demolition Path
1. **18-month delay** — certain, adds ~$712K in carrying costs
2. **Intense community opposition** — media coverage, Town Meeting pressure, possible emergency LHD proposal
3. **No tax credit eligibility** — lose 20%+ in rehabilitation incentives
4. **Larger buildable envelope** — more efficient site plan without preserving existing structure
